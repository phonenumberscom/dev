Backed by FCC rules

Porting is your right — no carrier can hold your number

Carriers must release your number

FCC rules require carriers to honor any valid port-out request. An unpaid balance or an active contract can’t be used to block the port — you settle those separately.

Simple wireless ports: one business day

That’s the FCC benchmark for a simple wireless-to-wireless port. VoIP and landline ports run longer, but they’re on a regulated clock too.

Your new carrier runs the port

You never ask your old carrier to port out. Hand your account number and PIN to the new carrier — they pull the number across while your line stays live.

Why ports get rejected

Five errors cause almost every rejection — and all five are avoidable.

  1. Cancelling service before the port completes

    A disconnected number goes back to the carrier’s pool — it can’t follow you. Cancel nothing until your new carrier confirms the port is done.

  2. A wrong or expired transfer PIN

    Most wireless transfer PINs expire within about a week. Request the PIN last, right before you submit the port — not weeks ahead.

  3. Name or billing ZIP that doesn’t match

    The details you give the new carrier must match the losing carrier’s records exactly — a maiden name, an old ZIP, or a business name on file will bounce the request.

  4. Using the phone number as the account number

    Nearly every carrier issues a separate account number — check the top of your bill or your online account. This is the single most common data error.

  5. A port freeze or account lock left on

    Security locks that stop fraudsters also stop your own port. Turn off any number lock or port freeze — or get the unlock passcode — before submitting.

Porting questions, answered

The rules are the same no matter which carrier you’re leaving.

What does “porting” a number mean?

Porting is moving your phone number from one carrier to another — you keep the number, callers notice nothing, and only the company billing you changes. It’s a federally protected right in the US and Canada, and it works the same whether the number lives on a cell plan, a VoIP app, or a landline.

Do I need my old carrier’s permission to port out?

No. Under FCC rules a carrier must honor any valid port request — you don’t need to call them, and you don’t need their approval. You only need your account number, transfer PIN, and matching billing details.

How long does a port take?

Simple wireless-to-wireless ports often complete the same day, with one business day as the FCC benchmark. VoIP ports typically take a few business days, and landline or cable ports can take one to four weeks.

Will my phone stop working during the port?

No — your number stays live with the old carrier until the moment the port completes, then service continues with the new one. Any overlap is typically minutes, not days.

Can I port with an unpaid balance or mid-contract?

Yes — the port itself can’t be blocked for either reason. You still owe the remaining balance or early-termination fee afterward; porting doesn’t erase it.

What is a transfer PIN and where do I get one?

A short security code that proves you authorized the port. Each carrier issues its own — usually in the account app, online dashboard, or by a dial code like AT&T’s *PORT. Find your carrier above for the exact path.

I bought a number from a marketplace — why is my carrier calling it a landline port?

Marketplace numbers are classified as wireline, so a wireless carrier processes the move as a landline-to-wireless port rather than a same-day simple port. Numbers we host with Bandwidth — the licensed carrier network behind many major US communications brands — clear to wireless carriers quickly, and your dashboard shows the exact account number and transfer PIN the new carrier needs.

What happens if my port is rejected?

Nothing is lost — your number stays active with your current carrier. Fix the field that didn’t match (PIN, account number, name, or ZIP) and resubmit. Almost every rejection traces back to one of the five mistakes above.
